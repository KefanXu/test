Project Scope
85.7% (50% of students think it’s very important) of the students who took the survey think it’s important for people in their field to have strong mentorship with renowned mentors. All the participants in this survey have experience of receiving mentorship. Most of them have two mentors over the years in their field of study.  Our survey also shows that contacting with the mentor personally is the most common way for students to find their mentors (57.1% of students would make contact with their mentors personally and 35.7% of students have their programs assigned mentors for them). When they are communicating with their mentors, most of the students would like to have a face-to-face meeting (85.7%) or send emails (64.3%). We find that students in their field rarely use online-platforms to connect with their mentors. Though there are some networking platform, like Linkedin, 71.4% of the students have never used such a website to connect with their mentors. None of them use web platform to communicate with their mentor.

User Flow
It is important for mentors and mentees in the medical field to be able to show off their mentorship connection to others. To address this problem, we created a prototype for a mobile app that would allow people to see mentorship connections on a graph. We also conducted some research into how to create a quanti- fiable score for a mentorship connection so that hiring organizations would be able to make use of this in their decision-making process.

Insights from the Affinity Wall
Due to the fact that we received both positive and negative feedback on having a scoring system quantifying the mentorship contributions of an individual, we decided to do two versions when creating prototypes, one with a scoring system - version 1, and one without - version 2. Version 1:When you first sign up, we would prompt to ask you whether you authorize our app to access your Phone and Email. Once your email and phone number are connected to our mobile app, we would run those data on the backend. We calculate the Mscore based on the interactions your mentor has with you (using the calculation method outlined in Dom et al). Through this version, a mentor is able to be assigned to a Mscore automatically.Version 1 is likely to have certain limitations due to current privacy laws. We strongly suggest that more review on privacy law needs to be done before deciding how the Mscore will be collected and calculated. We designed version 2 as a plan B - in case the privacy law forbids we collect user data, store them and use them for calculating Mscores. Version 2:In the notification center, for each mentor,  a notification pops up monthly to ask you to fill out a questionnaire. In the questionnaire, we ask you questions (question design would be based on the calculation method outlined in Dom et al) about the mentor from an objective perspective. We then use the information you provide to calculate the Mscore. Users would manually input this information that we otherwise need to collect through storing user data at the backend (demonstrated in version 1). In this way, we are able to avoid infringing data privacy.
The scoring system has some potential problems, including:May cause clustering that all mentees go to the mentor with higher score Makes people feel degrading Hard to be quantified
